Victorian Default Offer

How Effective is the Victorian Default Offer?

Author
Benjamin Willey, Victoria University
Date
21 February 2022

Introduction

The Victorian Default Offer (VDO) is a default electricity deal determined each year by the Essential Services Commission (ESC), Victoria’s independent energy regulator. The ESC claim that the VDO provides Victorian consumers access to a fair electricity deal. In this discussion, we examine whether this is indeed the case, and also look at whether the VDO is having an impact on the way Victorian electricity retailers are pricing market offers.

A residential electricity customer in Victoria is able to access market offers and standing offers. Standing offers are contracts that are provided when a customer has not engaged directly with the market – for example, by never signing up for an electricity contract, or by moving address and not entering into a new contract. Market offers are competitive offers set by the electricity retailers. There is no restriction to how often a retailer is able to set new market offers. The VDO was introduced on 1 July 2019 as part of the Victorian state government’s Energy Fairness Plan. It applies to all standing offers and is therefore the unique standing offer that will apply to a customer if they are unable or unwilling to engage in the market. In this discussion we will refer to such customers as reluctant customers. The stated intention of the VDO is to provide a simple and fair offer to reluctant customers. Approximately a month before the end of every calendar year since the inception of the VDO, the ESC produces a publicly available report setting out the methodology used to calculate the VDO for the following year as well as the VDO prices themselves. This occurs after a consultation period in which the electricity retailers and other stakeholders are invited to make submissions influencing the report. The VDO is calculated in a transparent manner (in the sense that it should be possible for an interested layperson to reproduce the calculation) and is made up of components accounting for wholesale costs, network costs, environmental costs, retail costs and other costs. To provide focus for our discussion we will look only at residential consumers within the CitiPower distribution region. In addition we examine only flat-tariff VDO and market offers. These are by far the most common offer type and will likely be most appealing to reluctant customers due to their simple structure. They include a daily supply charge (AUD per day) as well as a usage charge (AUD per kWh).

Questions

In 2017, the Independent Review into the Electricity and Gas Retail Markets in Victoria investigated how the then deregulated Victorian electricity retail market was functioning. The review found that the market was failing consumers and made 11 recommendations which were intended to improve outcomes for Victorian energy consumers. The first and most far reaching of these was the introduction of a Basic Service Offer, which later became known as the Victorian Default Offer. According to the Essential Services Commission website, the Victorian Default Offer:

...provides Victorian consumers access to a fair electricity deal even if they are unable or unwilling to engage in the retail market.

The review found three main factors for the perceived market failure which the VDO intended to address. These were:

  • The cost of competition
  • The structure of the market
  • The practices of industry

Therefore, the purpose of this working paper is to investigate the following questions:

  • Is the VDO providing a fair deal to consumers?
  • Has the structure of the market improved since the introduction of the VDO?
  • Have the practices of industry improved since the introduction of the VDO?

Data

We use three sources of data for our discussion, analysis of which has been undertaken using both the R and Python programming languages.

The first are the historical VDO prices which are all taken from the ESC website.

The second are historical fixed, flat-tariff market offers, for each electricity retailer operating in Victoria. These have been extracted from the Victorian Energy Compare website using web scraping software. We will refer to this data set as the offers data set.

The third are the market share classifications, in 2020, for each electricity retailer operating in Victoria. These were presented in the Victorian Energy Market Report published by the Essential Services Commission (ESC).

Discussion

A Fair Deal for Consumers?

The Victorian State Government webpage on the VDO states that:

the VDO is a fair offer but not necessarily the cheapest price for you

The Essential Services Commissions’ terms of reference and subsequent statements from the Premier and Minister for Energy, Environment and Climate Change (including the VDO Bill’s second reading in Parliament) also make clear that the purpose of the VDO is to provide customers with universal access to a ‘fair’ priced electricity offer.

There are two questions related to the fairness of the VDO:

  • Whether the VDO itself is a fair offer from a consumer perspective
  • Whether the VDO assists in creating a fairer market.

The Thwaites review notes that:

As an essential service, consumers must purchase energy and must participate in the retail market even if they are not interested in the product and regardless of continued price rises

This is undoubtedly true, but should we therefore conclude that the disinterested consumer not receiving the best prices is unfair? On the flip side, this means that the interested consumer is able to achieve lower prices – which could be considered the fairest situations depending on your point of view.

Indeed, fairness in the sense of equality is achieved by requiring that all standing offers be replaced with the VDO so that every customer on such an offer is treated equally regardless of which retailer they are with. The ESC has also achieved a sense of fairness by linking the calculation of the VDO to factors such as wholesale prices and reasonable retailer profit margins. However, many reluctant customers on the VDO who accept that they may not be getting the cheapest price for their electricity might be surprised to find out how poorly the VDO fares in comparison to market offers – even when the market offers selected are not from the cheapest retailers.

The Structure of the Market

The Thwaites review noted the differences between the large, medium and small retailers and noted that the large retailers were charging amongst the most expensive offers and took this as evidence that the more established retailers were not being put under competitive pressure by the medium and small retailers to lower their prices. They state:

If the competitive market was working we would not expect to see the low-cost supplier at the top of the price range and the high-cost supplier at the lower end

This is one of the identified market failures the VDO was supposed to address.
